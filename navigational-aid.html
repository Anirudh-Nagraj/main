Navigational Aid for runners who are blind - TRIDENT

PROJECT INFORMATION

  • Categories: Accessibility, Wearable Technology, User-Centered Design
  • User Base: Runners who are blind
  • Project Timeline: 2020 - January to December
  • Team Members: 4 students and 1 faculty

TRIDENT stands for Tri-Positional Detection and Navigational Technology. The key features of the system include enhanced awareness of surroundings, detection of obstacles above and below the waist, modes for long and short range detection and choice of output. These features have been determined from interviews conducted with blind joggers and runners

HELPING Emi

Meet Emi, a 38 year old runner who is visually impaired. He wants a device that can help him detect obstacles while running.

His main problem is with moving obstacles and ones at head height. He finds it hard to concentrate on the path because of the obstacles. To alleviate some of his problems, he approached us for help.

So we decided to build a device for him.

So what are Emi's base requirements?

Emi wants a device that relies on haptics and uses sensors to detect obstacles. He also wants it to be non-invasive and portable so that it does not hinder his run.

Research Questions

Emi gave his requirements, now we had a job on our hands. The time had come to create a list of initial research questions. Each member of the team came up with list of research questions and compiled later.

Whiteboarding

With the research questions now generated, our team sat together for round table discussions and whiteboarding. Keeping Emi's initial requirements in mind, we came up with a defined process, and a set of scenarios to aid future work.

Rolestorming

Let us put ourselves in Emi's shoes, shall we? The team enacted various scenarios involving individuals who are blind and running while coming up with different solutions in addition to the ones generated while whiteboarding.

Affinity Diagramming

Once we had all the data points ready, the team sat down and created an affinity diagram to draw themes and prioritize them. Collaboration was done on a tool called MIRO .

SOME INSIGHTS

  • Users cannot use the cane while running.
  • Multiple Obstacle detection is preferred.
  • Users want a non-intrusive device.
  • Users want a device that is multimodal.
  • Users would like the device to tell them about the obstacle well in advance.
  • Want something that is portable and lightweight.

Build

We were now at a stage where we could build the prototype for Emi. We chose 3 different sensors (Infrared, Ultrasonic, and LIDAR) to incorporate into the device, and from the research material gathered, we decided to go with a RUNNING BELT.

Prototype

The sensors are integrated in a way to mirror what we were working towards – A running belt worn by runners who are blind to detect obstacles. Velcro is used for ease of use. A headband is an add on to the device to detect obstacles at head height and has it's output generated on a wrist band to avoid head related issues.

We named the device, the TRIDENT. TRIDENT stands for Tri-Positional Detection and Navigational Technology.

Getting back to Emi, THE USP

We were ready to show Emi the prototype. But what sets our prototype apart and user oriented?

THE Unique Selling Point

This low cost device consists of a variety of sensors to offer a wider detection range. The output works on haptics and can be used by deaf-blind people as well. The users do not have to spend money on devices that are expensive and bear only one sensor as this prototype is a handy alternative.

Great, the prototype is ready. But we would like to know about the feasibility aspect of it and how it can be improved further ?

We wanted to check the feasibility of the prototype and conducted evaluation studies in the form of user interviews where we employed concept testing and gained useful insights..
