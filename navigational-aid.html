Navigational Aid for runners who are blind - TRIDENT

As a part of the Toyota Mobility Challenge, the project was a study to assess the manner in which blind users interact with technology while running and develop a prototype, to aid them with the running. The prototype was named TRIDENT, and was built from the insights obtained from research done from user interviews, and field studies. Analysis of existing applications and reviews were conducted to better understand how users react with respect to wearables in general.

WHAT IS TRIDENT?

TRIDENT stands for Tri-Positional Detection and Navigational Technology. The key features of the system include enhanced awareness of surroundings, detection of obstacles above and below the waist, modes for long and short range detection and choice of output. These features have been determined from interviews conducted with blind joggers and runners.

IMPACT

This low cost device consists of a variety of sensors to offer a wider detection range. The output works on haptics and can be used by Deaf and Hard of hearing people as well. The users do not have to spend money on devices that are expensive and bear only one sensor as this prototype is a handy alternative . We anticipate that people in Low-and Middle income countries would be able to afford the device should it hit the market. This device can be used for regular walking as well, extending from the runners . All users liked the idea of a non-intrusive belt that can be used to detect obstacles. We have made this belt as non-intrusive as possible. Majority of the users feel that the belt is lightweight and can be used as an alternative to the devices currently available.

RESEARCH ISSUES

While there is a lot of buzz in the market related to accessibility related products with respect to individuals who are blind, there is hardly any device that pops up when specific to running and obstacle detection.

There is not enough documented evidence to suggest that the existing research aids blind runners.

Efficiency of many prototypes yet to be tested in real world environments.

It is still unknown whether users would be able to connect and run the setup successfully should there be a reassembly or a first time assembly.

Ease of access and wear of the prototype among prospective users.

RESEARCH STRUCTURE

To build the prototype, the team divided the user research into four segments. The first segment involved reviews and analysis. The second segment involved profiling the user base and the third segment consisted of populating insights for prototype building , the last segment was the evaluation of the built prototype.

Segment #1 - Review and Analysis

For the review and analysis phase of the user research, the team performed competitive analysis, conducted one-one interviews with users to uncover specific insights based on running and prototyping.

Literature Reviews

The team collected several works related to prototypes for individuals who are blind. This rigorous search helped the team to understand the development from other researchers' perspectives. I populated and read through research papers to gain insight and information on the given topic, identify gaps in previous research. Overall, we wanted to have a solid background for our study.

Insights

  • Research related to running is at a premium. Researhers have focussed on aiding individuals who are blind while walking from one place to another.
  • Research indicates the presence of more than one kind of sensors located at different regions to tackle dynamic obstacles.
  • An array of sensors is used as a feedback mechanism post obstacle detection.
  • Research relates to augmenting the experiences of the white cane or building a device in conjunction with the cane.
  • The literature study also helped provide an overview of some of the specifics used in assistive devices (Sensor information etc.,). Seeing that many researchers built prototypes as walking assistance and not running was considered as a mini milestone for our project.

Competitive Analysis

The team looked at different products available on the market. The competitive analysis enabled us to go through the strengths and weaknesses of the various devices available in the market today, used by people who suffer from vision impairments. Trying out the devices gave an understanding of the features and functions and informed the design process. User reviews helped us understand what the users think about the different products.

STRENGTHS

  • The BuzzClip is a portable device with a single sensor that is extremely Portable.
  • SUNU Band is a wrist band like device that works on sensor feedback and is integrated with a mobile application.
  • Devices are built for a niche audience and serve their purpose wel. Good connectivity to bluetooth and GPS, enabling efficient detection of obstacles.

WEAKNESSES

  • High learnability curve among some of the devices present ensures hardships among the users.
  • High pricing of devices may prevent the users from buying the same.
  • Devices like the BuzzClip have a clip on option. They risk falling off and go unnoticed.
  • The minimum pricing is in the higher $200 range.

OPPORTUNITIES

  • Expanding the usage from normal walkin to other activities.
  • Reducing price range to attract wider audience.
  • Integrating with existing applications to allow greater usage.
  • Providing sensor and haptic information via audio cues to the user to enable efficient usage of the device.

THREATS

  • Increased number of competitors in the market.
  • Learnability levels.
  • Not decreasing the cost may prevent users from buying the devices.
  • Complexity in feedback may prove to be a hindrance.
  • Reliance on the white cane.

Segment #2 - Profiling the users and their environment

Having understood the competitive landscape, research projects, and the behavior of users while on the run, we were ready to profile the users, build empathy and solidify the information we had at our disposal. The team decided to build storyboards, scenarios, rolestorming, to come up with the solution for the problem under consideration.

BodyStorming

Bodystorming was done to get an idea on the possible scenarios with the prototype to be designed. Equipment like a helmet, wires for pathing were used to demonstrate how the potential user may feel/think/act in a given scenario. Bodystorming sessions were also done to generate empathy for runners who are blind.

Insights

  • The potential outcomes for bodystorming meant that we knew where we would struggle in terms of scenarios and where a possible solution may lie. For instance, we tried tugging when an obstacle came in close proximity to the actor. Tugging was difficult to pull off practically even though it was good theoretically.
  • One thing about body storming is that researchers must be aware of the sensitivity of the topic under consideration without which the bodystorming session can go completely wrong.

    • Scenarios

    Multiple scenarios were developed over a period to ensure that major situations are covered and the prototype addresses them. We believe that by creating the scenarios, we could generate empathy for our user base (runners who are visually impaired) and design, and develop the most optimal solution. Scenarios also made us understand the key features of our device, where it may be used and also shed some light on the environmental condition that may hamper it's usage. We created scenarios because we believed that it would help us in understanding environmental specific motivational factors and guide user thought process.

    Insights

  • Empathy: Scenarios guided us in the design process in the sense that it allowed us to empathize with the users related to specific situations.
  • Granularity of information: How much info does the user get? Can they explore Points of Interest? Can they learn about unusual occurrences (e.g. construction, large slopes, weird intersections)?
  • Granularity of feedback: How much feedback is the device giving? Is it long, auditory instructions
  • Level of interaction: How much focus does the user need to have on their device? Do they need to signal it whenever they want info, or is info automatically given to the user?

    • Storyboards

    Once there was a good understanding of the needs and the potential uses of a device through bodystorming and the persona and scenario development, we put that information into a visual storyboard. After the completion of the storyboard the entire team was able to easily understand the product design as well as the features that were to be included and we were able to start identifying the necessary materials to build and test this prototype.

    PROTOTYPE SELECTION AND DEVELOPMENT

    We were now at a stage where we could build the prototype based on the insights. We chose 3 different sensors (Infrared, Ultrasonic, and LIDAR) to incorporate into the device, and from the research material gathered, we decided to go with a RUNNING BELT. As one of the two prototype developers on the team, I set myself a deadline to get everything up and running. Within two months, it was ready for review.

    The sensors are integrated in a way to mirror what we were working towards ‚Äì A running belt worn by runners who are blind to detect obstacles. Velcro is used for ease of use. A headband is an add on to the device to detect obstacles at head height and has it's output generated on a wrist band to avoid head related issues.

    We named the device, the TRIDENT. TRIDENT stands for Tri-Positional Detection and Navigational Technology.
