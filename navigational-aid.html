Navigational Aid for runners who are blind - TRIDENT

PROJECT INFORMATION

  • Categories: Accessibility, Wearable Technology, User-Centered Design
  • User Base: Runners who are blind
  • Project Timeline: 2020 - Present
  • Team Members: 4 students and 1 faculty
  • My Role: Lead User researcher and prototype developer

TRIDENT stands for Tri-Positional Detection and Navigational Technology. The key features of the system include enhanced awareness of surroundings, detection of obstacles above and below the waist, modes for long and short range detection and choice of output. These features have been determined from interviews conducted with blind joggers and runners.

HELPING Santiago

Meet Santiago, a 38 year old runner who is visually impaired. He wants a device that can help him detect obstacles while running.

His main problem is with moving obstacles and ones at head height. He finds it hard to concentrate on the path because of the obstacles. To alleviate some of his problems, our team at the University of Maryland, Baltimore County decided to build a device for him through the user centered approach . The project was done under the watchful eye of Dr.Ravi Kuber.

So what are Santiago's base requirements?

Santiago wants a device that relies on haptics and uses sensors to detect obstacles. He also wants it to be non-invasive and portable so that it does not hinder his run.

Assumptions

We made some assumptions before the team started the whiteboarding process. Some of the assumptions varied from the runners like Santiago running regularly, using tethers while running, using existing technology, finding technology usage difficult while running, etc.,

Research Questions

Santiago gave his requirements, now we had a job on our hands. The time had come to create a list of initial research questions. Each member of the team came up with list of research questions and compiled later.

Whiteboarding

With the research questions now generated, our team sat together for round table discussions and whiteboarding. Keeping Santiago's initial requirements in mind, we came up with a defined process, and a set of scenarios to aid future work.

Rolestorming

Let us put ourselves in Santiago's shoes, shall we? The team enacted various scenarios involving individuals who are blind and running while coming up with different solutions in addition to the ones generated while whiteboarding.

Affinity Diagramming

Once we had all the data points ready, the team sat down and created an affinity diagram to draw themes and prioritize them. Collaboration was done on a tool called MIRO .

SOME INSIGHTS

  • Multiple obstacle detection: Participants want an array of obstacles to be detected and not just a single obstacle.
  • A non-intrusive device that is multimodal: Participants do not want a device that impedes them in any way either physically or mentally in any way while running
  • Presentation of multiple haptic feedback: Participants are looking at receiving an array of feedback in the form of haptics while approaching an obstacle.
  • Detection of obstacles: Participants look forward to learning about the obstacle from a short distance away.
  • Weight Constraints: Participants do not want to carry something heavy while running as it impedes their run.
  • Terrain: Participants want the device to work in multiple terrains, and not just track and field.

Build

We were now at a stage where we could build the prototype for Santiago. We chose 3 different sensors (Infrared, Ultrasonic, and LIDAR) to incorporate into the device, and from the research material gathered, we decided to go with a RUNNING BELT. As one of the two prototype developers on the team, I set myself a deadline to get everything up and running. Within two months, it was ready for review.

Prototype

The sensors are integrated in a way to mirror what we were working towards â€“ A running belt worn by runners who are blind to detect obstacles. Velcro is used for ease of use. A headband is an add on to the device to detect obstacles at head height and has it's output generated on a wrist band to avoid head related issues.

We named the device, the TRIDENT. TRIDENT stands for Tri-Positional Detection and Navigational Technology.

Getting back to Santiago, THE USP

We were ready to show Santiago the prototype. But what sets our prototype apart, and what makes it user oriented?

Unique Selling Points

This low cost device consists of a variety of sensors to offer a wider detection range. The output works on haptics and can be used by deaf-blind people as well. The users do not have to spend money on devices that are expensive and bear only one sensor as this prototype is a handy alternative.

Great, the prototype is ready. But we would like to know about the feasibility aspect of it and how it can be improved further ?

We wanted to check the feasibility of the prototype and conducted evaluation studies in the form of user interviews where we employed concept testing and gained useful insights..

Takeaways

From the start to finish, this project has been a learning curve, and we are still on the process of refining the prototype. I realized that empathy generation is key because of the type of audience involved. In addition to that, I also learned to employ the human aspect in research. For without connection and interaction with people, I believe that nothing is possible. Figuring out the research techniques and drawing the right insights is a challenge in itself. Additionally, as we are dealing with a niche audience, getting people for interviews and observation was also a challenge. Here is hoping that this prototype becomes a product someday.
